Swordwhales of the Thermocene

Swordwhales are significant ocean predators in Serinan seas by the Themocene, with smaller species surviving successfully through the acidification crisis at the end of the Cryocene and fairly rapidly evolving back into large predatory forms. Descended distantly from the Xiphophorus fishes (platies and swordtails) they fill the niche of sharks on Earth and broadly resemble a cross between a shark and a barracuda. Swordwhales have large, extensible jaws lined with dozens of sharp teeth, which are replaced throughout their lifetimes, and they are without exception carnivorous. The smallest species would fit into a modest home aquarium, while the largest are monstrous apex predators, some of whom can reach lengths as long as a school bus, and surely are capable of hunting almost anything they wish to in the sea. Swordwhales have large eyes and unlike sharks are predominately visual hunters. Most species feed predominately living prey and are fast and agile swimmers. They will feed on carrion if they come across it, but only a small percentage make a habit of regularly scavenging as, also unlike sharks, most of them lack a particularly keen sense of smell.

Swordwhales were named for the appearance of their earliest representatives, some of which utilized an elongated lower tail fin to thrash through shoals of bait fish and stun them. Such forms, however, went extinct at the Cryocene-Thermocene boundary. The surviving groups which reclaimed the open seas were more generic small shallow-water predators which never developed such an adaptation. Modern hunting techniques usually rely on either ambush from cover or from underneath sediment, in which cases the fishes have developed flattened bodies and highly reduced buoyancy, or active pursuit in fast, agile open water predators, with the prey being caught in the jaws which are highly elastic and can be shot forward at rapid speed to grab prey. Swordwhales are ecologically diverse and make use of many different diets, however, in addition to fish; some groups have evolved crushing molars to smash shellfish, while others have even become parasitic, biting and twisting away small circular plugs of tissue from other large fishes. The largest species is an active predator, but species only marginally smaller have adopted filter-feeding techniques; their teeth absent or vestigial, they cruise along the surface and suck up whole shoals of copepods and shrimp with massive gapes.

With relatively large brains, the swordwhales could be considered intelligent among fishes. Some are even social, not simply in a shoaling way, but working in groups to hunt. They cooperate in team to corral shoals of fish into tight formations which can be more easily managed. There even existed a lineage of large, social swordwhale in which the female which guarded its young in the safety of a pack until they were large enough to fend for themselves. Unfortunately, this group did not survive the end of the Cryocene, with modern representatives migrating to sheltered coastal environments to birth their young before abandoning them to their fate. The babies are born entirely capable to fend for themselves and proportionally large to their mother in litters which can range from just a couple in some large species to ten or more in the smaller types. All swordwhales nourish their developing young via a placental tie, the first among the fishes on Serina to develop such a method of providing nourishment to their young after they left their eggs and thus to allow them to develop to a much greater size before leaving the mother's body.

Males have also developed a beneficial reproductive adaptation by evolving a penis; their gonopodium fin, used ancestrally to transfer their sperm and fertilize females, has become a mobile, extendable tentacle. Through a tube on the inside, their genetic material is transferred directly inside the female's body in true internal fertilization.
